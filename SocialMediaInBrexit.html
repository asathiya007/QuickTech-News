The Impact of Social Media on Brexit

Akshay Sathiya - February 19th, 2019

Summary

  • Brexit is the event in which the United Kingdom/Great Britain decided to leave the European Union, which followed the referendum of 23 June 2016 and which will officially start in March 29, 2019.
  • Many people believe that social media had a huge role in the development and passing of Brexit. Those in favor of Brexit were supposedly higher in both number and activity on social media than those who were in favor of the UK/Great Britain remaining in the European Union. This greater social media presence not only made the viewpoints and beliefs of those in favor of Brexit louder in volume, but also farther in its span. Those in favor of Brexit had much more influence over the voters in the middle ground and were able to sway them to vote in support of Brexit.
  • The for-Brexit side of the election did a much better job than the against-Brexit sie of the election when it came to using hashtags. By using three simple hashtags (#Brexit, #Beleave and #VoteLeave) over and over in their posts, Brexit supporters were able to connect with each other and also connect middle ground voters to other Brexit supporters and pro-Brexit content. Ultimately, the for-Brexit side of the election was able to amplify the Brexit support and spread it to the middle ground voters in such a way that the against-Brexit side of the election was not able to, which ultimately sealed the election and allowed Brexit to pass.

Implications

  • Hashtags are a powerful of connecting one's views and beliefs to other users who share said views and beliefs and users who are in the middle ground and can be swayed toward a certain political alignment given enough perceived support and content pertaining to a certain political event.
  • In the very possible case that certain news outlets and/or sources provide inaccurate or false information (intentionally or unintentionally), this could falsely sway voters to vote on a political issue in a way that they do not agree with or understand, which delegitimizes not only the popular vote, but can also steer government in the wrong direction in what it believes is best for its constituents.
  • Young people who are relatively new to politics and political involvement can be made the targets of aggressive social media campaigns. Given that young people are the most active generation on social media, this creates a combined scenario in which the next generation of voters and politicians can be easily and heavily swayed by current political groups and ideologies when they are in a stage of their lives and careers where they are not as experienced or knowledgeable as they should be in order to make the big decisions that they need to. Ultimately, young people are more likely to be targeted by the polar political groups and campaigns, and this is a focus that social media companies such as Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, etc. should take into account when considering the experiences and impacts that their respective platforms will have on their young users.
