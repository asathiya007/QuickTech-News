Google Assistant - On a Button?

Akshay Sathiya - February 25th, 2019

Summary

  • Google Assistant is a smartdevice voice assistant built and maintained by Google. It allows users (owners of Android smart devices) to ask questions, schedule events, make calls, etc.
  • Initially, Google Assistant could only be accessed via the home button of the smartdevice, but now it has its own access point. It has its own button on certain smartdevices where users can directly access and use the Google Assistant
  • Some devices which have implemented the Google Assistant button are the LG G8 ThinQ and K40 as well as the Nokia 3.2 and 4.2. There are also a variety of Samsung devices which have implemented stand-alone buttons for the Samsung voice assistant, Bixby.

Implications

  • This may be a response to a growing desire of easy-access voice control in the smartdevice consumer space. Having a standalone button for accessing a voice assistant will allow users to engage with their technology through a more vocal manner rather than a physical one. If this technology grows quickly, it could dictate how users use their smartdevices, which would in turn dictate how future smartdevices will be designed.
  • This could represent a huge stride from Google into the voice recognition and IoT sectors of the tech industry. Smart home hubs like Google Home and Amazon Echo are already making it big, and Google's acquisition of Nest connects more home machines. Having a google assistant available at the push of a button will be another way that Google is pushing their voice assistant and IoT technologies into the consumer space and into the palms of their individual consumers. This could also be a way for them to integrate mobile devices into the IoT scheme.
  • Although there are many ways in which such a design change can impact the industry and the lifestyle of modern consumers, it is important to understand that this could very well fall flat. As one of Android's biggest consumers, Samsung may try to push its own voice assistant, Bixby, into the fray, and may not support Google and Android as one of their biggest clients. Common consumers could also not see a need or advantage to having their Google voice assistant available by the push of a button. Why would it be any different, since they would have to be able to speak to it anyway once it is turned on? This may be revolutionary, but if the consumers to do not see it as such, then this step made by Google may not have any effect in the long run.
